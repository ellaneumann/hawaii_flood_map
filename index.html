How the Score Is Calculated
Each cell on the map gets a Flood Risk Score from 0 to 1 (0 = no risk, 1 = extreme). The score is a weighted average of 9 physical factors — each factor is measured, normalized to 0–1, then multiplied by its weight below. Drag the sliders to change how much each factor counts, then hit Recalculate to update the map.

Factor Weights — Total: 100%
15%
Low-lying land floods first. Coastal plains and valley floors score highest (near 1); mountain ridges score near 0.
7%
Flat ground ponds water (high risk); moderate slopes drain quickly. Very steep slopes produce dangerous flash flows to areas below.
15%
O'ahu ranges from 18 in/yr (leeward Ewa) to 200+ in/yr (windward Ko'olau). More rain = more flood events per year.
12%
Pavement and rooftops block water from soaking in. Downtown Honolulu is 75–85% impervious — nearly all rain becomes surface runoff.
11%
Rated A–D by the USDA (United States Department of Agriculture): A soils drain fast (low risk), D soils drain very slowly and saturate quickly (high risk). Coastal alluvial soils are almost all D.
15%
Distance to the nearest stream channel. Areas within 0.5 km score near 1; beyond 8 km scores near 0. Kaukonahua Stream flooded homes in Waialua during the March 2026 event.
10%
Former pineapple plantation soils (Waialua and Ala soil series) contain 18–28% iron oxide (Fe₂O₃). This cements a hard subsurface layer that blocks water from draining down, forcing it sideways into streams — raising flood peak flows by 30–50% vs. sandy soils.
10%
Ground-truth layer: areas where flooding, structural damage, or rescues were actually documented during the March 2026 Kona Low storms. A high score here means real flood events confirmed the model's predictions.
5%
Whether this area was under an official civil-defense evacuation order or warning March 20–22, 2026. Issued by Honolulu Civil Defense based on dam failure risk and stream overflow.

Methodology: Weighted multi-factor vulnerability index using SCS Curve Number concepts. Elevation from USGS 1/3" DEM control points, rainfall from Hawaii Rainfall Atlas, soils from SSURGO/NRCS, impervious surface from NLCD, streams from NHD. Grid cells ~500m. This is a screening-level analysis — not a substitute for calibrated hydraulic modeling.

Limitations: Simplified DEM interpolation, no subsurface flow modeling, no storm drain network. Best used for relative comparison between areas and scenarios, not absolute flood depth prediction.

Context
Why was this so catastrophic?
Saturated ground: Storm 2 hit soil already at capacity from Storm 1. Even moderate rain caused immediate runoff and flooding.

Aging infrastructure: The Wahiawa Dam was built in 1906. North Shore drainage systems were not designed for this volume.

Radar gap: The Molokai WSR-88D radar was offline, removing a key tool for real-time forecasting during the event.

Climate signal: Kona lows are a normal feature of Hawaii's winter, but this back-to-back sequence with tropical moisture at this intensity is consistent with climate-change-driven intensification of Pacific weather systems.
