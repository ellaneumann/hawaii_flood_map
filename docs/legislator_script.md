# One-Minute Legislator Script
### Hawaii FloodWatch · Ka Moamoa Lab

---

> *Read at a measured pace. Aim for 60 seconds. Pause briefly after each paragraph.*

---

In March 2026, 236 people were pulled from rooftops on O'ahu's North Shore. 5,500 families evacuated because a dam built in 1906 — for sugar cane — nearly failed in a modern storm.

This map shows why that happened.

Every color you see represents a flood risk score based on nine real, measurable factors: elevation, soil drainage, how close you are to a stream, how much concrete covers the land — and one factor that does not show up on FEMA's maps: iron oxide in the soil. Dole and Del Monte pineapple operations spent decades compacting North Shore soils into a hardpan layer that blocks rain from absorbing into the ground. That compaction is still there. It is still accelerating runoff into streams by 30 to 50%. The plantations are gone. The flood risk they created is not.

Here is what matters for your vote: when you click the Indigenous Land Restoration scenario on this map, the high-risk areas shrink. Not hypothetically. Based on measured data from restored loʻi kalo sites in Hawaii, returning taro wetland agriculture and ahupuaʻa watershed management to these lands demonstrably reduces flood peaks. Communities managed these same watersheds without catastrophic flooding for over a thousand years.

We are asking for two things. First, emergency funding for the communities and infrastructure that March 2026 exposed as dangerously underprepared — the North Shore, the aging dam network, the storm drain systems built for a plantation economy.

Second, sustained investment in Native Hawaiian land stewardship programs. Not as a cultural gesture. As flood infrastructure. The data is here. The map shows it. The only question is whether we act before the next storm does.
